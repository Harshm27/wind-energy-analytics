A Data-Driven Business Case for Kaluza

Executive Summary

This report evaluates the business opportunity for Kaluza, a UK energy technology company, to reduce wind energy curtailment in Scotland's Orkney Islands through strategic Demand Response (DR) programs. Our analysis quantifies that approximately 217 GWh of renewable energy is curtailed annually across 500 wind turbines due to grid constraints and insufficient local demand.

Through comprehensive data analysis of 2+ years of turbine telemetry and residential demand patterns, we demonstrate that a DR program targeting 50% household participation (~5,192 households) could significantly reduce this waste while creating value for wind farmers, residents, and grid operators. Our findings provide actionable insights for program design, pricing strategy, and stakeholder engagement.

1. Objectives and Project Approach

In order to evaluate the business opportunity for Kaluza, we address three fundamental questions:

  1. How much energy is currently curtailed annually across the Orkney Isles?
  2. How much can this be reduced by different levels of DR penetration?
  3. How many local households would need to be on our DR scheme to supply this level of DR?

More broadly, this report details the business case of curtailment reduction on the Orkney Islands. At the core, the report:

  • Analyzes supply-side dynamics through wind turbine telemetry data
  • Examines demand-side patterns via residential consumption data
  • Models demand-supply matching opportunities
  • Develops pricing and revenue-sharing frameworks
  • Provides strategic recommendations for program implementation

2. Supply Analysis: Wind Energy Generation

2.1 Data Overview

Our supply analysis focuses on wind turbine telemetry data from a representative 900 kW turbine in Rousay, one of the Orkney Islands. The dataset contains:

  • Timestamp: Minute-by-minute records from 2016-01-01 to 2018-01-01
  • Power_kw: Actual power output at each timestamp
  • Setpoint_kw: Target output set by control system (curtailment indicator)
  • Wind_ms: Wind speed measurement

2.2 Data Quality & Preprocessing

We implemented rigorous data cleaning procedures:

  • Missing Values: Removed 2015 data due to extensive gaps (187 of 218 days missing)
  • Temporal Consistency: Verified 98% of data at consistent 1-minute intervals
  • Outlier Removal: Excluded anomalous readings where high wind speeds (>20 m/s) produced unexpectedly low power output (<800 kW)
  • Validation: Confirmed power output never significantly exceeded setpoint beyond measurement tolerance (~17.67 kW average overshoot)

2.3 Seasonal Patterns

Time series analysis revealed clear seasonal trends in wind power generation:

  • Peak Generation: Spring months show highest output
  • Lowest Generation: Mid-to-late summer experiences significant drops
  • Winter Performance: Stable output around annual median

Key Insight: Wind Speed-Power Relationship

Wind turbine power output generally increases with the cube of wind speed, making small velocity increases highly impactful. Our predictive models leveraging this relationship achieved R² > 0.9, enabling accurate potential energy estimation.

3. Curtailment Estimation

3.1 Methodology

We developed a comprehensive methodology to quantify energy curtailment:

  1. Predictive Modeling: Built forecasting models using wind speed to estimate maximum potential power output
  2. Curtailment Calculation: Computed difference between potential maximum and actual output (governed by setpoint)
  3. Energy Integration: Converted power (kW) to energy (kWh) by integrating over 30-minute intervals
  4. Geographical Scaling: Adjusted for wind speed variations across 9 island locations
  5. Fleet Extrapolation: Scaled from single turbine to all 500 turbines

3.2 Key Assumptions

  • Turbine Population: ~500 turbines across Orkney (source: Orkney Renewable Energy Forum)
  • Turbine Capacity: 900 kW rated capacity
  • Geographic Distribution: Evenly distributed across 9 community-owned wind farms (~60 turbines per site)
  • Wind Variations: Historical annual average wind speeds used to adjust estimates per location

4. Demand Analysis: Residential Consumption

4.1 Data Overview

Our demand analysis utilized residential energy consumption data covering 10,385 households across Orkney Islands for the year 2017. The dataset provided 30-minute interval demand measurements, enabling detailed pattern analysis.

4.2 Consumption Patterns

Daily Patterns

  • Morning Peak: Sharp rise around 8:00 AM (household preparations)
  • Evening Peak: Second surge around 7:00 PM (evening routines)
  • Off-Peak: Midday trough and overnight lows

Seasonal Variations

  • Summer: Lowest demand (reduced heating needs)
  • Winter: Highest demand (increased heating and lighting)
  • Spring/Autumn: Moderate, transitional demand levels

4.3 Demand-Curtailment Alignment

A critical finding emerged from comparing curtailment and demand patterns: curtailment peaks during summer months when residential demand is lowest. This inverse relationship highlights both the challenge and opportunity for DR programs — shifting flexible loads to high-generation periods can materially reduce waste.

5. Demand Response Modeling

5.1 DR Potential Calculation

We estimated total DR potential based on controllable household loads:

  • Controllable Loads: Heating systems, electric vehicles, water heaters, smart appliances
  • Controllability Factor: 50% (conservative estimate based on industry standards)
  • Total DR Potential: 121,504 kW × 0.5 = 60,752 kW

5.2 DR Penetration Scenarios

We modeled four DR penetration levels to assess curtailment reduction:

DR Level Target Reduction (kW) Impact on Demand
10% DR 12,150 kW 10% monthly demand reduction
20% DR 24,301 kW 20% monthly demand reduction
30% DR 36,451 kW 30% monthly demand reduction
50% DR 60,752 kW 50% monthly demand reduction

Environmental & Economic Impact (50% DR Scenario)

  • Annual Energy Avoided: 22.17 million kWh
  • CO₂ Savings: ~6,652 tonnes annually
  • Grid Cost Avoidance: ~$9.1 million per year

6. Participation Requirements

6.1 Household Recruitment Targets

To translate DR targets into operational thresholds, we calculated required household participation:

Households Required = (Total Annual Demand × DR Level) / Average Household Annual Demand

6.2 Implementation Roadmap

We recommend a phased rollout approach:

  • Phase 1 (Year 1): Pilot with 1,000-1,500 households (10-15% DR) to test systems and refine offerings
  • Phase 2 (Year 2): Scale to 3,000 households (30% DR) based on pilot learnings
  • Phase 3 (Year 3+): Target 5,000+ households (50% DR) for maximum impact

The 40-30-30 split (Wind Farmers - Kaluza - Residents) provides optimal balance:

  • Sufficient incentive for wind farmers to participate
  • Sustainable margins for Kaluza platform operations
  • Attractive savings for residents to drive adoption

8. Conclusions & Recommendations

8.1 Key Findings Summary

  • Significant Opportunity: ~217 GWh of renewable energy curtailed annually represents substantial value capture potential
  • Feasible Scale: 50% household participation (5,192 households) can deliver meaningful curtailment reduction
  • Inverse Patterns: Curtailment peaks in summer when demand is lowest — DR programs must incentivize strategic load shifting
  • Environmental Impact: 50% DR scenario saves 6,652 tonnes CO₂ annually
  • Economic Viability: Multiple revenue-sharing models support sustainable business case

8.2 Strategic Recommendations

For Kaluza

  1. Phased Rollout: Begin with 10% DR pilot (1,000 households), scale to 50% over 3 years
  2. Balanced Pricing: Adopt 40-30-30 revenue split with $1.00/kW payout as baseline
  3. Technology Investment: Develop user-friendly mobile app for automated load management
  4. Stakeholder Engagement: Build partnerships with wind farmers and local government early
  5. Data Infrastructure: Implement real-time monitoring to optimize curtailment-demand matching

For Policy Makers

  • Provide regulatory support for DR programs in island communities
  • Offer subsidies or grants for pilot programs demonstrating curtailment reduction
  • Streamline data-sharing between grid operators, wind farms, and DR platforms

For Residents

  • Participation reduces energy costs while supporting local renewable energy
  • Automated systems minimize lifestyle disruption
  • Contributes to Orkney's sustainability leadership

8.3 Future Research

Further analysis could explore:

  • Battery storage integration to complement DR programs
  • Commercial and industrial demand response opportunities
  • Cross-island load balancing through smart grid technologies
  • Long-term wind generation forecasting under climate change scenarios

Final Verdict

Our analysis demonstrates a compelling business case for Kaluza to pursue demand response programs in Orkney Islands. The combination of significant curtailment, feasible participation targets, and multiple viable pricing models positions this as a high-impact, sustainable opportunity in the renewable energy transition.
